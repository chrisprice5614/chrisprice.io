If You Hear This Song, Someone Dies

Day of Wrath

The Dies Irae is an infamous piece of music written sometime in the medeival period. The translation means "Day of Wrath," and describes the day of judgement. Historically the chant would be sung during a Roman Funeral Mass. Many composers have taken either musical themes or text from their chant for their own works. Mozart's Dies Irae is a popular example of another composer using ideas from the original Dies Irae.

The translation of the text is as follows:
Day of wrath and doom impending! David's word with Sibyl's blending,
Heaven and earth in ashes ending!
Oh, what fear man's bosom rendeth, When from heaven the Judge descendeth,
On whose sentence all dependeth.
Wondrous sound the trumpet flingeth; Through earth's sepulchres it ringeth;
All before the throne it bringeth.

Enter Berlioz

Young French Composer, Hector Berlioz, popularized the Dies Irae by using it to tell a story, rather than a funeral setting. In his Symphonie Fantastique, Berlioz mixed the tune with other themes from the same symphony to represent the death and torment of the main character in the symphony. At the time this made many religious people upset, because the chant was also played along-side a vulgar dance tune version of the symphony's main theme.

Berlioz' symphony was extremely popular, making the Dies Irae used by other composers to represent death in a more secular manner. In the 1980 film, The Shining, the Dies Irae is used throughout the film as the main theme, representing death.

Composition Popularity

Composers of all different styles start using Dies Irae in their music to represent deaths. In films we see Dies Irae used as just one single motif, giving a hint to observant audience members that a character will die or has died. Lizst would commonly use the phrase to represent the death of a character in his music.

It's become somewhat of an easter egg to look for and find the dies irae in music. Due to the invention of the internet and the accessibility of music creation tools, the Dies Irae is used by young composers more than it ever has before.
